# Organizer Handbook

The _Organizer Handbook_ is an educational and referential resource for WCA competition organizers of all levels of experience, whether you are organizing your first competition or your fiftieth. This document lays a foundation for the responsibilities and most common scenarios experienced by organizers so that every WCA competition can run with consistent levels of fairness and quality.

Organizers work alongside WCA Delegates to make competitions happen. Delegates are responsible for ensuring the WCA Regulations are followed, submitting results, and writing Delegate Reports. This handbook covers the organizational responsibilities that fall to the organization team. For Delegate-specific responsibilities, see the [Delegate Handbook](wcadoc{edudoc/delegate-handbook/delegate-handbook.pdf}).

While this handbook aims to be complete, it cannot be fully comprehensive. As you organize more competitions, you will build experience that allows you to handle scenarios not covered here. For additional detail on specific topics, see the [Organizer Guidelines](https://www.worldcubeassociation.org/organizer-guidelines) and the [Additional Organizer Resources](#additional-organizer-resources) section at the end of this document.

Finally, the _Organizer Handbook_ aims to reflect official WCA policy, not create it. When a disagreement or contradiction arises, always defer to the official policy, Regulation, or precedent published by the WCA. See the [Contributing to the Organizer Handbook](#contributing-to-the-organizer-handbook) section for information on how to update this handbook to accurately reflect reality.

There is a lot of information that is relevant to all WCA Volunteers, not just organizers. If you have not already, read the [WCA Volunteer Crash Course](wcadoc{edudoc/volunteer-crash-course/volunteer_crash_course.pdf}) to get a high-level overview of the responsibilities expected of every WCA Volunteer.

# Introduction to Organizing {.page-break-before}

Organizing a WCA competition takes significant time and effort. The organization team is responsible for most of the practical work of preparing for and running a competition, including finding a venue, managing registrations, coordinating volunteers, and ensuring competitors have a positive experience. Delegates handle WCA-specific duties such as generating scrambles, ensuring Regulations compliance, and submitting results.

Work closely with your listed Delegates throughout the organizational process. They can guide you through the process, approve competition details, and help resolve issues on competition day.

For guidance on forming an organization team and contacting a Delegate, see the [Creating Your Organization Team](wcadoc{edudoc/organizer-guidelines/orga-team-delegate.pdf}) document.

To understand how organizers and Delegates typically share responsibilities, see the [Community Organizers and Delegate Organizers](wcadoc{edudoc/delegate-handbook/delegate-handbook.pdf}) section of the Delegate Handbook.

## WCA Competitions Are Not for Profit

WCA competitions are **not for profit**. Do not organize a competition for personal gain. Delegates may decline to delegate competitions that appear to be organized for profit.

# Before the Competition {.page-break-before}


Organizers have many responsibilities before a competition happens. Work with your Delegates to ensure every organizational task is completed before the competition begins. Work with your Delegates when planning competition frequency, size, and event selection. For guidance on fostering the health of your local cubing community and regional competition planning, see the [Competitions and Your Community](wcadoc{edudoc/delegate-handbook/delegate-handbook.pdf}) section of the Delegate Handbook.

For detailed guidance on specific topics such as finding a venue, creating a budget, or handling registrations, see the [Additional Organizer Resources](#additional-organizer-resources) section.

## Communicating with Competitors and their Families

It is generally a good idea to send emails to competitors before the competition to encourage them to make the necessary updates to their registration and remind them to plan for the competition. Remember to **always use the BCC field in communications with multiple competitors** so that other competitor emails are not shared. The WCA website will automatically add all recipients to the BCC field if you email them by checking the box next to their registration on the Registration page of the competition website and then clicking the “Email” button. If there is a data breach, notify your Delegates immediately so they can contact the WRT as soon as possible and explain the situation.

See the [Emails](wcadoc{edudoc/organizer-guidelines/emails.pdf}) document for more information on emailing competitors.

## Generating Groups

It is the organization team’s responsibility to generate groups. Your Delegates are responsible for ensuring groups are fair and consistent. The following events are considered highly scramble-dependent, as the scramble alone can have a strong impact on who achieves a podium placement or advances to subsequent rounds:

- 3x3x3 Cube
- 2x2x2 Cube
- 3x3x3 Blindfolded
- 3x3x3 One-Handed
- Clock
- Pyraminx
- Skewb
- Square-1

For non-first rounds, it is a good practice to put all of the similarly skilled competitors in one group to ensure that they receive the same scrambles. Use the results of the previous round to determine which groups to place competitors in.

Tools such as [Groupifier](https://groupifier.jonatanklosko.com/) can help with generating groups. Coordinate with your Delegates to ensure the groups meet their fairness requirements.

# Day of the Competition {.page-break-before}


On competition day, the organization team is responsible for running the practical aspects of the competition. Delegates ensure the competition follows WCA Regulations and handle incidents, penalties, and results submission.

## Setup and Environment

The organization team or venue employees are typically responsible for setting up the competition room. Ensure that the competition layout abides by the WCA Regulations, which are outlined in [Article 7: Environment](https://www.worldcubeassociation.org/regulations/#article-7-environment). In addition, [Regulation 1c](https://www.worldcubeassociation.org/regulations/#1c) outlines that your competition must follow all applicable WCA policies and requirements. This includes the [WCA Scramble Accountability Policy](https://documents.worldcubeassociation.org/documents/policies/external/Scramble%20Accountability.pdf). Pay especially close attention to zoning. The scrambling area must be visually isolated from all areas that could have competitors not involved in organizing or scrambling the ongoing group.

Your Delegates will review the competition setup and make any changes necessary to make it fully compliant with WCA policies. For detailed guidance on venue layout, see the [Venue Setup](wcadoc{edudoc/organizer-guidelines/venue-setup.pdf}) document.

## Check-In and Registration

Most competitions will have a check-in or registration table. Place this table close to the main entrance of the competition room so that competitors entering can easily pick up any necessary materials for the competition, such as a name tag and lanyard. Only exclude a check-in table if your Delegates agree it is unnecessary—for example, when the competition is very small, has few or no first-time competitors, and your Delegates can easily identify every competitor without one.

Printouts with private information, such as a first-time competitor checklist with dates of birth, must remain private and **never** be left unattended at a check-in table. Such printouts contain sensitive information and must not be shown to competitors.

For more information on registration management, see the [Handling Registrations](wcadoc{edudoc/organizer-guidelines/registration.pdf}) document.

### On-the-Spot Registration

On-the-spot registration policies must be clearly stated in the Extra Registration Requirements on the competition website. Follow these general rules:

1. Competitors must not be accepted on the day of the competition under any circumstances unless on-the-spot registration for the competition is enabled.
2. If registration is full, on-the-spot registrations _must not_ be accepted on the day of the competition.

Some regions have created alternative waiting list formats that maximize the number of competitors who compete and reduce no-shows. The Extra Registration Requirements must clearly outline these policies to be allowed. Here are some examples of acceptable formats that have been approved in the past when the process was explicitly outlined in the Extra Registration Requirements:

1. Competitors on the waiting list are added in the order of the waiting list after the waiting list deadline if the conditions are specified.
2. Competitors attempting to register on the spot when registration is full may be added on the day of the competition in the order of arrival when on-the-spot registration is enabled if the conditions are specified.
    1. On-the-spot registrations must not be accepted before competitors on the waiting list unless otherwise specified.
3. If an online waiting list is not used and registration is full, competitors attempting to register on the spot may be added on the day of the competition in the order of arrival when on-the-spot registration is enabled, if specified.
4. Other policies may be accepted at the discretion of the WCAT.

Above all, the competitor limit must not be exceeded for any reason. Competitor limits will not be increased after a competition if registrations are accepted beyond the specified limit.

## First-Time Competitor Tutorial

A first-time competitor tutorial is recommended to help reduce incidents when there are first-time competitors at your competition. The tutorial is typically held at the start of the competition or right before the 3x3x3 event. Depending on your competition schedule, you could hold the new competitor tutorial at another time during the competition to maximize its impact. The durations vary by region. Most first-time competitor tutorials take around 10 to 20 minutes.

- If the first event of the competition is unlikely to have many first-time competitors, you may choose to delay it until right before the first first-time competitor-friendly event, as some first-time competitors might not be present for events they are not competing in.
- If there is a day when few first-time competitors are expected, such as a day focused on blindfolded events, you could skip the tutorial entirely and hold it on another day.
- If there are potentially multiple days that a first-time competitor could attend for the first time, consider holding multiple first-time competitor tutorials on each day of the competition.

Coordinate with your Delegates to schedule a first-time competitor tutorial. When a Delegate cannot deliver the tutorial, such as when they do not speak the local language, you or a trusted competitor may deliver it instead. If there are many first-time competitors, it might be best to have multiple separate tutorials so that everyone can see and hear their tutorial more easily.

The following points are an outline of a first-time competitor tutorial for a WCA competition. Depending on the specific events of the competition, it might be a good idea to have a secondary judging tutorial before an event that has specific judging instructions. For example, 3x3x3 Blindfolded judging does not have the judge time the inspection phase and has them hold a sight blocker. In the Clock event, competitors are allowed to manipulate the pins during the inspection, judges must check both sides of the puzzle, and the puzzle does not have any +2 misalignment penalties. Modify the tutorial as necessary to fit the needs of your competition.

- How to read a nametag, or otherwise determine the competitor’s competing, judging, scrambling, and/or running groups.
- What to do when the competitor’s group is called.
- How to compete:
  - Telling the judge they are ready.
  - Inspection time, including penalties.
  - How to start the timer properly:
    - Hand position, particularly using fingers to start the timer.
    - Waiting for the green light on the timer.
  - How to stop the timer properly:
    - Flat hands (e.g. no “karate chops”).
  - How to sign for attempts.
    - Check that the time matches the timer and is written legibly. Times that are not written legibly will be interpreted in the worst way possible. Competitors are allowed to ask their judge to re-write times if they are hard to read.
  - How to call over a Delegate.
- How to judge:
  - Asking the competitor whether they are ready and waiting for the competitor’s ready signal.
  - Judging the inspection, including lifting the puzzle cover, operating the stopwatch, and making callouts.
    - Emphasize that only “8 seconds” and “12 seconds” are called out, not 15 or 17 seconds.
    - Emphasize that they should only stop the stopwatch when the competitor starts their solve so that the actual time of the inspection is measured.
  - What to look for when the competitor starts their solve, including:
    - Timer start hand position.
    - Whether the competitor is touching the puzzle.
  - How to judge during the solve:
    - Pay attention. Do not cube, use your phone, or engage in conversation.
    - Do not distract the competitor during the solve. Sit still and be quiet.
    - Watch the competitor for any Regulations violations.
    - Pay close attention to how the competitor stops their timer.
        - Ensure they are using their hands with palms flat and facing down.
  - Finishing the solve.
    - The first step is to evaluate whether the cube is solved.
        - Do not touch the puzzle. Use only your eyes.
    - How to evaluate the cube for +2s:
        - Explain that a single turn (e.g. U or U') is a +2 penalty on an NxNxN puzzle.
        - Explain that a “double move” (e.g. U2 or R2) is also a +2 penalty and not considered two separate moves.
        - Explain that a slice move (e.g. M or M2) is considered two outer moves and is thus a DNF.
        - Tell them that if the cube is close to the limit for a +2 penalty (e.g. 45 degrees on NxNxN puzzles), and the judge cannot determine the final state, they should call over a Delegate to make the final decision.
    - Explain that a corner twist is a DNF.
    - Explain how missing pieces affect the solved state.
        - The complexity of Regulation 5b5 can make this difficult for competitors to understand. Emphasize that judges may call over a Delegate if they are uncertain about how to rule specific missing pieces.
    - How to write the final time.
        - Emphasize legible handwriting.
    - How to write the final time when there are penalties.
    - Ask the competitor to sign for the attempt.
  - What to do after the attempt is over. This can vary depending on how your competition is being run.

For additional guidance on working with first-time competitors, see the [Attitudes to First-Time Competitors](wcadoc{edudoc/organizer-guidelines/new-competitors.pdf}) document.

## Preparing for Subsequent Rounds

If your competition has more than one round of an event, you will need to prepare for the subsequent rounds. This includes generating groups for the next round, ensuring sufficient judges and scramblers are available, and updating the schedule as needed.

When creating groups for subsequent rounds, put similarly skilled competitors together for scramble-dependent events (see the [Generating Groups](#generating-groups) section).

Coordinate with your Delegates when preparing subsequent rounds, as they must verify that groups are fair and that any schedule changes comply with the Regulations.

## Modifications to the Schedule or Events

If you run behind or ahead of schedule, discuss adjusting the schedule of upcoming events with your Delegates to recalibrate with the flow of the competition. Any modifications to competition details (e.g. advancement conditions, time limits, cutoffs, or number of rounds) must be approved and implemented by a Delegate, who must update these details on the WCA website and synchronize WCA Live.

### Running Behind Schedule

Running behind schedule is the most common schedule deviation at WCA competitions. Discuss the following options with your Delegates to try to get the competition back on schedule:

- Continuing the competition as-is and ending later than expected
- Reducing the allotted time for lunch
- Tightening advancement conditions
- Reducing cutoffs or time limits
- Removing rounds

Discuss any schedule changes with your Delegates before implementing them. Your Delegates must consider the fairness of such changes under the WCA Regulations and will determine which options are appropriate.

### Running Ahead of Schedule

The stakes are lower when you are running ahead of schedule. Simply continuing ahead of schedule will allow for a buffer if there is a delay later in the competition and allows you to leave the venue early if you finish ahead of schedule. Some competitors travel a long way to get to competitions and will appreciate being able to get a head start on their journey home. Alternatively, if it is still the morning, consider extending lunchtime, especially if it is under one hour. Having additional time to get food, eat, relax, and warm up before the competition resumes could greatly improve the competition experience.

If you still want to fill up some time and have longer events with a cutoff time, discuss with your Delegates whether to increase the cutoff time rather than the time limit. Increasing the cutoff time can give some competitors a better chance of getting a full mean or average, resulting in longer rounds.

Discuss with your Delegates whether to increase the number of competitors who advance to a subsequent round or add an additional round. Remember that some competitors might have already left the venue if they were not expecting to advance to a subsequent round.

If competitors arrive based on the online schedule while the competition is running ahead, notify a Delegate so they can determine how to handle the situation.

For more information on schedule planning, see the [Scheduling a Competition](wcadoc{edudoc/organizer-guidelines/schedule.pdf}) document.

# After the Competition {.page-break-before}


## Lost and Found

Competitors frequently lose personal items, particularly puzzles, during a competition. If there are unclaimed items at the end of a competition, coordinate with the Delegates on what to do with them. You can try to return lost items to their owners, although that is not always feasible or possible. It is appropriate to include a mention of lost items in post-competition communications. Instead of listing the missing items, encourage people to reach out with a description of the item to prevent people from claiming items that don’t belong to them.

## Record Keeping

According to the WCRP, digital records related to competition finances must be retained for at least seven years. Establish processes to maintain financial records before the competition. Check requirements for financial record-keeping for the government where the competition was held, as local requirements could exceed the WCA’s seven-year expectation.

For guidance on post-competition reflection, see the [Reflection](wcadoc{edudoc/organizer-guidelines/reflection.pdf}) document.

# Contributing to the Organizer Handbook

The _Organizer Handbook_ is an ever-evolving resource and is subject to change as the WCA and the cubing community change. Every WCA Volunteer is encouraged to contribute. If you want to fix, improve, or otherwise change the document, it can be found in the [WCA Documents GitHub repository](https://github.com/thewca/wca-documents). If you already know how to use Git and GitHub, the best way to contribute is by opening a Pull Request or an Issue. Otherwise, send a message to the WCA Quality Assurance Committee at [quality@worldcubeassociation.org](mailto:quality@worldcubeassociation.org) and describe the changes you would like to propose.

Due to the length and complexity of this document and the need to update it semi-regularly, there is currently no option to create official translations of the Organizer Handbook. Since the language used for communication within the WCA is American English, it is expected that most organizers can read this document.
